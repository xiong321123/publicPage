PREFACE

We live in different times from when our grandfathers lived in, who lived in different times from their grandfathers, but as you move back in time, this same rule of life changing rapidly does not hold true.

We can broadly divide the history of human civilization in three separate parts: the agricultural age, the industrial age, and the digital age.

At first, we started as stone age men who lived in caves and jungles. Life was hazardous and hard (at least, by today’s standards) but we eventually learnt to clear up land and grow our own food.

We learned to breed crops for high yield and also learned to domesticate animals. This was the agricultural age.

When people think of the men and women of these times, they think that these people were a different species – more ape-like, possessing lower intellect and less nuanced emotions than us.

This, of course, is not true. As you read this, I want you to remember something: the people who lived in these times were just as smart, just as clever, and had exactly the same emotional range we do today. They were modern homo sapiens in every way.

A great many of them were, in fact, cleverer than us and achieved a lot more with much less knowledge and technology. When people hypothesize that the pyramids were built by aliens and giants, they do not give the men who came before us enough credit.

Back to the agricultural age: Man settled down around cultivable land and formed society. This is when people started to specialize. Instead of everyone being a hunter gatherer, there were farmers, herders, teachers, healers, potters, etc.

This went on for a long time and the world didn’t change much for millennia. You could go forward a thousand years during this era and things would pretty much be the same. The son lived the same life as his father, who lived the same life as his father, and so on - and this went on for hundreds if not thousands of generations.

Remember that these men were just like you - just as smart, clever, and with the same emotional range. They just hadn’t figured out how to create modern technology.

8

They had no idea about the vastness of the world and an individual person didn’t have much power or influence unless they were kings.

The options the average non-noble person had in his life were very limited

- you could either till the land, provide some sort of service, or fight in the king’s army. The labor was not scalable, and even if you were the best and brightest - there was limited social and economic mobility.

Then came the industrial age. It would be fair to say that everything changed.

Using machines, one man could do the work of a dozen (if not a hundred or a thousand) and this brought the world untold amounts of wealth and prosperity.

Unlike the bygone days where everyone was a small enterprise tilling the land or selling some service or handmade product, now men would work in factories for a wage and make a pretty good income.

If you were the best and brightest, you could be an inventor and become rich from your own business. You no longer had to be an born in an aristocratic family to be rich - you just needed to be smart, invent something useful, and find a financier to set up a factory and hire a bunch of men.

Business became scalable. Instead of making one pot, you can now manufacture thousands of pots using machines and sell them using distributors. Instead of painstakingly copying a book by hand, you now had the printing press that will print you as many copies as you can sell.

New products arose, and prices of things went down over time. Everything was in flux. Men lived much better than their fathers and grandfathers as technology kept improving rapidly.

Tractors became better, harvesters showed up in the market, the clothes were better, the work was different, factories became more and more efficient… in short, all the machines got better, and human life with it.

Things were so different that a producer from the agricultural age could not make do after the industrial age took hold. This is very different from the agricultural age itself where you could go a thousand years and everything is pretty much the same. This is for a few reasons: By this time, he could no longer just clear up some forest land and become a farmer. That era was long gone and all the real estate in the world was controlled by some government or the other.

The work of an artisan like making and selling pots or sewing clothes had been rendered economically uncompetitive by machines.

9

Outside of joining the standing army, a man pretty much had two choices: he could either start a business or he could work for a wage.

Businesses are of two types:

• Non-scalable business, like a doctor’s or a lawyer’s practice. This typically involves selling time for money. It’s better than working for a wage, but you’re not getting rich. There’s a limited number of customers you can serve because it is a localized and personalized service.

• Scalable business. This typically involves a standardized product being produced at large scale and sold to large numbers of people.

Hours worked is not directly correlated with income and the people who found a way to scale made untold amounts of money.

An example of a scalable business is the manufacture of the monitor you’re reading this on. Some company manufactures it in large numbers and sells it all over the world. The guy who owns that company doesn’t spend his time selling individual units. His work is pretty much the same whether he sells a million units or two million units.

During the industrial age, it was still quite difficult to start a scalable business - even if you had a great product or idea. This was because of two key constraints: capital and labor.

Capital: Setting up a factory costs, hiring a lot of people, setting up a supply chain, and spending on advertising to educate the market about your product costs a lot of money. It can be a herculean task to just find the money to get started! You might need to sell a large percentage of your business before you’ve even begun, and it is unlikely that you would find the funds you need even if you were willing to do so.

Labor: Skilled labor is always in short supply, and even with unskilled labor you have to deal with egos and expectations. You have to manage people, or you will have strikes, theft, and intentional property damage.

It was back in the industrial age that the then richest man in the world John D. Rockefeller famously said that the ability to deal with people is as purchasable a commodity as sugar or coffee and I will pay more for that ability than for any other under the sun.

These were the issues that plagued entrepreneurs from back in the day, and the large needs of capital prevented most of them from ever getting to try their ideas out.

Today, we are in the digital age. The invention of the globally connected network of computers (i.e. internet) changed everything.

Instead of needing to hire thousands of workers, paying them, and dealing with strikes, complaints, and management hassles, you can now use software to do the same work cheaply, efficiently, and with no errors.

In the digital age, there are no barriers to entry. A large amount of capital isn’t required - websites, domains, digital order fulfillment, social media etc. are all either free or cheap (much cheaper than the computer you’re reading this from), and you don’t need a lot of human labor because of software.

This is the age of entrepreneurship where anyone who wants to create something can do so.

The Long Tail

When the tools of production are available to everyone, everyone becomes a producer.

— Chris Anderson

The long tail was an expression popularized by Chris Anderson in an October 2004 Wired magazine article to describe certain types of business and economic models such as Amazon or Netflix. Anderson elaborated on the concept in his book The Long Tail: Why the Future of Business Is Selling Less of More. The term long tail is commonly used in statistics in relation to wealth distributions or vocabulary usage.

"The Long Tail theory holds that our culture and economy is shifting from being focused on a small number of 'hits' (widely accepted products and markets) at the top of the demand curve, to being dedicated to a huge number of specialized niches in the tail," explains Chris Anderson.

The long tail is a business model that refutes traditional models in which it was taught that the products that should be sold are those with the highest turnover.

John wants to increase his income, but he only sees two ways, either he increases the price of his classes, or he gives more classes.

Considering that the price would be limited by the competition and the number of classes that he can offer is limited to the hours of the day minus his resting hours. Given these factors, even if he is a great coach, would limit his income. That is, it is a not very scalable system.

Thinking about how he could make his business more scalable, he considers opening a gym so that he can offer more services. But, going this way, his expenses would increase, he would have to pay the rent of the premises, the necessary refurbishment, buy or rent the equipment, and the personnel salaries.

John, intending to avoid such a large outlay, thinks of another option.

He would generate demand by offering nutrition and training advice on YouTube, Instagram, and Twitter.

He would create a podcast where he would talk about training and nutrition.

And he would offer his courses online and sell them through Shopify.

Obviously, this would be a tremendous effort. But the upfront cost is very low. And he could also build it progressively, even combining it with his current personal training until the business takes off.

Since the cost of a new customer signing up for his monthly service is practically zero, John sets a lower monthly rate than that of his personal training classes at $25/month.

Because of the variety of structure and application, fallacies are challenging to classify to satisfy all practitioners. Fallacies can be classified strictly by either their structure or content, such as classifying them as formal fallacies or informal fallacies, respectively. The classification of informal fallacies may be subdivided into categories such as linguistic, relevance through omission, relevance through intrusion, and relevance through presumption.

On the other hand, fallacies may be classified by the process by which they occur, such as material fallacies (content), verbal fallacies (linguistic), and again formal fallacies (error in inference). In turn, material fallacies may be placed into the more general category of informal fallacies, while formal fallacies may be clearly placed into the more precise category of logical (deductive) fallacies. The life of human beings for the vast majority of the time we have lived on this planet has been plagued with enormous risks and threats, this has resulted in evolution rewarding a very risk-averse brain.

Progress, especially in recent centuries, has diminished if not eliminated those risks. Being attacked by a predator, being without shelter to protect ourselves or without food are practically non-existent threats in today's society.

Our environment is much safer than what our internal wiring is prepared for. Our world changes faster than our biology. We are wired to survive in an environment that ceased to exist thousands of years ago.

This has made us give up our freedom for security. But this has come at a cost to our happiness and our physical and mental health, since we are forgetting the values that make us human: to create freely, adding value to society.

The Internet has made these values more readily available than ever before in human history.

The opportunity: Technology has made it easier than ever to achieve these values.

The cost: getting them has a price, managing to add value to society.

If something defines us humans, it is the ability to create complex tools.

Everything you see around you, from cars to cell phones, has been created by people.

Creating is in our DNA and we feel good when we create, as the internal reward system rewards us, and we feel empty when we don't.
